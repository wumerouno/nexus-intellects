Why Your CV Is Not Getting Responses

A CV can fail even when the candidate is qualified. The issue is often not your experience. It is how clearly your value is positioned for the role you want.

Nexus Intellect Editorial July 2, 2026 6 min read
In this guide Your CV is not targeted enough You describe duties instead of achievements The document is hard to scan The right keywords are missing Your professional profile is too vague How to fix the response problem

Recruiters do not read CVs like novels. They scan for fit, evidence, relevance, and risk.

Your CV is not targeted enough

One of the biggest CV problems is trying to use one document for every opportunity. A general CV often becomes too broad to be compelling. It lists experience, but it does not guide the recruiter toward a clear decision.

Your CV should reflect the role, sector, seniority, and value proposition you are pursuing. The headline, profile, skills, achievements, and work history should all point in the same direction.

You describe duties instead of achievements

Duties show what you were assigned. Achievements show what changed because you were there. A CV that only lists duties makes it hard for employers to see impact.

Where possible, use numbers, scale, tools, outcomes, revenue, cost savings, process improvements, customer results, project size, or team contribution. Even if you cannot use exact figures, you can still describe the result clearly.

Weak

Responsible for social media pages.

Stronger

Managed content calendar and improved page engagement through consistent campaign posts.

Weak

Handled customer service.

Stronger

Resolved customer enquiries, escalated recurring issues, and improved response consistency.

The document is hard to scan

A recruiter may spend only a short time deciding whether to keep reading. Long paragraphs, crowded formatting, unclear dates, inconsistent headings, and decorative templates can make a qualified person look disorganised.

Use a clean layout, strong section headings, readable spacing, and concise bullets. The CV should make relevance easy to find.

The right keywords are missing

Many employers and recruitment systems search for role-specific language. If your CV does not include the tools, competencies, industry terms, and responsibilities tied to the job, it may not surface even when you have relevant experience.

This does not mean stuffing the CV with random keywords. It means translating your experience into the language of the role you want.

Your professional profile is too vague

The profile section should quickly establish who you are, what you are strong at, and the kind of value you bring. Lines like hardworking, passionate, and able to work under pressure do not differentiate you.

A stronger profile names your function, experience level, sector exposure, core strengths, and the outcomes you support.

Function

What kind of professional are you?

Evidence

What experience or track record supports the claim?

Strength

What are your strongest relevant capabilities?

Direction

What kind of role or contribution are you positioning for?

How to fix the response problem

Start by choosing the role direction. Then rewrite the CV around evidence, relevance, and clarity. Remove information that distracts from the target role. Strengthen weak bullets. Update your LinkedIn profile so the public version of your career matches the document you are sending.

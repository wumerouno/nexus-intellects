What Investors Look for in a Business Proposal

A business proposal is not just a request for money. It is an argument that your business understands a real market, can execute, and can use funding responsibly.

Nexus Intellect Editorial July 2, 2026 7 min read
In this guide A clear problem and a real customer A market that can support growth A business model that makes sense Numbers you can defend Team strength and risk awareness Make the proposal decision-ready

Funders do not invest in enthusiasm alone. They look for evidence, clarity, logic, and execution capacity.

A clear problem and a real customer

Investors first want to know whether the business is solving a problem that matters. The proposal should explain the customer, the pain point, the current alternatives, and why the timing is right.

Avoid describing the idea only from the founder perspective. Show the reader what the customer experiences and why your solution is relevant enough for people to pay attention, switch behaviour, or spend money.

A market that can support growth

A strong proposal gives funders confidence that there is enough demand to justify investment. This does not mean throwing around huge market-size numbers without context. It means showing a realistic understanding of the market you can actually reach.

Define your target segment, location, buyer profile, demand drivers, competitors, and route to market. The proposal should show that you know where the opportunity is and how your business can access it.

Target customer

Who pays, who influences, and who uses the solution.

Demand driver

Why the need exists now and why it will continue.

Competition

What alternatives exist and where your edge sits.

Route to market

How the business will reach, sell to, and retain customers.

A business model that makes sense

Investors need to understand how the business makes money. That includes pricing, sales channels, customer acquisition, margins, repeat purchase potential, and the operational costs required to deliver the product or service.

The model does not need to be complicated. It needs to be believable. A proposal becomes weaker when revenue assumptions are disconnected from customer behaviour, production capacity, pricing reality, or the cost of growth.

Numbers you can defend

Financial projections are not magic tables. They are a structured explanation of what you believe can happen and what assumptions support that belief. A serious reviewer will look at whether your sales, costs, margins, and timelines make sense together.

Good projections should be ambitious enough to show opportunity and realistic enough to survive questions. If you cannot explain where the numbers came from, the document will struggle.

Revenue assumptions

Show price, volume, customer growth, and sales channels.

Cost structure

Include production, operations, staff, marketing, logistics, technology, and admin.

Funding use

Explain exactly what the requested capital will fund.

Milestones

Link money to measurable progress.

Team strength and risk awareness

Funders want to know who will execute the plan. Founder experience, team competence, advisers, partners, and operational readiness all matter. The team section should show why this group can do the work.

A credible proposal also names risks. Market risk, regulatory risk, supply risk, pricing pressure, execution gaps, and adoption challenges should not be ignored. A proposal that recognises risk and explains mitigation feels more serious than one pretending nothing can go wrong.

Make the proposal decision-ready

The strongest proposals are easy to review. They use clear headings, direct language, structured evidence, realistic figures, and a logical flow from problem to opportunity to plan to ask.
